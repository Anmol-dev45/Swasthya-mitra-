Technical architecture — offline-first model

Nepal's rural connectivity is patchy — Kaski, Gandaki, and most hill districts have 2G/3G at best. The app must work 100% without internet and sync intelligently when signal appears. Here is the exact architecture to use.

Core principle

Local-first, cloud-backup

The device is the primary database. The cloud is a sync destination — not a dependency. Every feature works offline. Sync happens automatically in the background whenever signal is detected.

Why not Firebase / pure cloud?

Cloud-first fails in Nepal

India's NCD-GoI app (the closest comparison) crashes in poor connectivity. FCHVs in rural Kaski or Dolpa may go 3–5 days without stable data. A cloud-first approach destroys the field experience and loses data.

Layer-by-layer architecture

Layer 1 — Local storage: SQLite via Drift (Flutter)

All patient records, BP/glucose readings, CVD risk scores, referrals, and follow-up schedules stored in a typed SQLite database on the FCHV's Android phone. Uses the Drift package — type-safe SQL in Flutter. Encrypts sensitive data at rest using AES-256 (SQLCipher). Data never leaves the device until sync is triggered.

↓

Layer 2 — Sync queue: Workmanager background tasks

Every write creates a sync queue entry with a UUID, timestamp, and operation type (create / update / delete). Flutter's Workmanager package runs a background task every 15 minutes. When connectivity is detected (via connectivity_plus), it pushes queued records to the cloud in compressed JSON batches. A sync-status indicator (green dot / grey dot) shows the FCHV when data is uploaded.

↓

Layer 3 — Conflict resolution: last-write-wins + server merge

If a supervisor edits a record while the FCHV is offline, conflicts are resolved with a timestamp-based last-write-wins policy for simple fields (BP readings, follow-up dates). For complex cases (referral status updated by both FCHV and health post), the server stores both versions and flags for manual review. This mirrors how Medic Mobile handles conflicts for Nepal's maternal health FCHVs.

↓

Layer 4 — Cloud backend: Supabase (PostgreSQL + REST + Realtime)

Supabase is the ideal backend for a Nepali health startup — open source, HIPAA-ready, deployable on AWS Singapore (low latency to Nepal), and free up to 500MB. Supervisor dashboards pull from Supabase in real time. Anonymised aggregate data feeds the analytics module. Row-level security enforces that FCHVs only see their own ward's data.

↓

Layer 5 — Supervisor & analytics: Web dashboard (Next.js)

Dr. Priya's supervisor dashboard is a web app (Next.js + Tailwind), not a mobile app — it runs on her laptop or office desktop. It connects to Supabase, shows real-time sync status, maps high-risk wards, and exports NHMIS/MoHP-compatible CSV and PDF reports. No separate dashboard app to maintain or train users on.

Revenue model — three streams to profitability

Swasthya Mitra is a B2G SaaS platform (Business-to-Government) with a secondary B2B analytics tier. The FCHV app is always free to the FCHV — revenue comes from the municipalities, province health offices, and development organisations that deploy it. This is the same model used by Medic Mobile and DHIS2 globally.

Why municipalities will pay

The budget already exists

Nepal's 2017 federalisation gave municipalities their own health budgets. Ward offices regularly allocate NPR 5–15L/year for NCD screening month (Falgun), FCHV equipment, and health programmes. Swasthya Mitra replaces paper, reduces travel costs for supervisors, and generates the MoHP reporting data they are legally required to submit. It is a budget line they already have — you are replacing how they spend it.

The B2G sales motion

Government sales is slow — plan for it

A municipality procurement cycle takes 3–6 months. Your sales motion: (1) Run a free 3-month pilot with 1 FCHV ward. (2) Generate a one-page impact report showing screening numbers and BP control rates. (3) Present at the quarterly health coordination committee. (4) Get included in the next annual health work plan budget. Budget approval cycles in Nepal run April–July for the new fiscal year starting Shrawan (mid-July). Target contract signing by Poush (December).

Competitive moat — why this is hard to copy

A mobile app alone is not a moat. What makes Swasthya Mitra defensible is the combination of government relationships, community data, and embedded workflows that become stickier over time.

1
FCHV network lock-in
Once 500+ FCHVs are trained on your app and their patient records live in your database, switching to a competitor means retraining 500 people and migrating years of patient data. The switching cost is not technical — it is human. This is the same moat that Medic Mobile has maintained for 15 years across Africa and Asia.
2
Municipal budget integration
Once you are a line item in a municipality's annual health work plan (and Nepal's new federalised system makes this the procurement route), you renew automatically with the fiscal year. Getting IN is hard; getting OUT is harder for the government than for you.
3
NCD patient data — a national asset
Within 3 years, Swasthya Mitra could hold the most comprehensive longitudinal NCD dataset in Nepal — hypertension and diabetes trends by ward, district, and province, with follow-up outcomes data. This anonymised dataset is valuable to MoHP, WHO, academic researchers, and pharmaceutical companies. It is a moat no new entrant can replicate from scratch.
4
MoHP / IHIMS integration
If you become the first app officially integrated with Nepal's IHIMS (Integrated Health Information Management System), you become the default — not by law, but by inertia. Every subsequent digital health initiative in Nepal will build on your schema, not replace it.
5
Nepali-language UX depth
Building a truly low-literacy, Nepali-voice-guided experience for FCHVs takes 18+ months of field iteration. An Indian competitor (the most likely entrant) will struggle with cultural fit, language nuance, and FCHV workflow specifics. This UX depth is earned through iteration — it cannot be bought.
8

Risk register — and how to mitigate each

HIGH
Government procurement cycles are slow
Municipalities may take 6–12 months to formally procure. Mitigation: Start with a free pilot MOU (not a contract). Generate outcomes data during the free period so that when the budget cycle opens, you have a proven case. Never depend on government revenue in your first 18 months.
HIGH
FCHV digital literacy gap
FCHVs are WhatsApp users but not app users. A complex interface will fail. Mitigation: Maximum 3 taps to complete any core action. Full Nepali voice guidance on every screen. 3-day hands-on training (not a manual). Peer-champion model: train 5 "super FCHVs" per ward who support their peers.
MEDIUM
Data privacy and community trust
Patients in rural Nepal may distrust digital health records. Mitigation: No patient photos or identifiers stored. Data encryption disclosed in plain Nepali. Community consent sessions before any ward launch. FCHV as the trusted messenger — not a faceless app.
MEDIUM
Phone hardware fragmentation
FCHVs own a wide range of cheap Androids (Samsung, Realme, Vivo, Tecno). Sync bugs and UI glitches vary by device. Mitigation: Test on 8+ device models before launch. Standardise on Android 8+ minimum. Consider a subsidised device programme (1 per ward) funded by municipality budget if needed.
MEDIUM
Donor dependency risk
If 60%+ of revenue comes from one donor in Year 2, you are vulnerable to grant non-renewal. Mitigation: Treat every donor project as a municipality sales pipeline — use the project to sign 3+ permanent municipal contracts. Target 50%+ government SaaS revenue by Year 3.
LOW
MoHP policy change / competitor entry
Unlikely in the short term given the gap in the market. Mitigation: Engage MoHP's eHealth division from Month 3 — not to get permission, but to co-design. If MoHP eventually builds their own tool, your data integration and FCHV adoption make you the natural implementation partner rather than a displaced competitor.
Bottom line
This is a NPR 20L bet that could return NPR 3–5Cr by Year 3 — and save tens of thousands of lives in between.
The technology is straightforward (proven Flutter + SQLite stack). The market is captive (50,000 FCHVs with no digital tool). The funding path is clear (Nepal Startup Fund → UNICEF/WDF grants → municipal SaaS). The only variable is execution — building the right product with the right field partners, earning government trust one municipality at a time, and letting the outcomes data do the selling for you.
