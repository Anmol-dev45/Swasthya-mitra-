Business model & money Q1 – Q6 · ~3 minutes
BusinessHard
"Who exactly pays you? The FCHVs are volunteers. Patients are rural poor. Where does the money actually come from?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
Municipalities pay. Nepal's 2017 federalisation gave all 753 municipalities their own health budgets and legal authority to procure health services. The ward office, not Kathmandu, is our client. They're already spending NPR 5–15 lakh per year on Falgun NCD Screening Month — paper registers, manual data, FCHV stipends. We replace the paper-based spend with our platform for the same budget line. The FCHV app is free forever. The municipality pays for the dashboard, reports, and data infrastructure. FCHVs don't pay. Patients don't pay. Government pays — because they're legally required to do NCD reporting and currently doing it badly.
"The FCHVs are the product users. Municipalities are the customers. The budget already exists — we're redirecting it, not creating it."
BusinessHard
"NPR 5–8 lakh per municipality per year. 753 municipalities total. That's a ceiling of NPR 60 Cr — and you'll never get all 753. This isn't venture scale. Why should I care?"
~30 sec▾
Answer
Municipality SaaS is the beachhead, not the ceiling. Three layers above it: Province health offices pay NPR 20–30 lakh for aggregate analytics — 7 provinces. MoHP-level national licensing for NHMIS integration. And the real scale play: regional replication. Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Cambodia — all have community health worker networks, zero unified NCD digital tool. A $15–20K licensing fee per country deployment gets us to $1–2M ARR without rebuilding the product. Nepal is the proof of concept. Medic Mobile proved the same model in Ethiopia, then licensed globally. That's our path.
"Municipality SaaS is our wedge. Province analytics and regional licensing are our scale. Nepal is the pilot; South Asia is the market."
BusinessHard
"Government procurement in Nepal takes 6–18 months. How do you survive while waiting for your first cheque?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
We deliberately don't depend on government revenue in Year 1. Our survival is grant-funded: Nepal's Startup Fund offers up to NPR 25 lakh — non-dilutive, disbursed in 3–6 months — which covers our entire MVP build. In parallel, WHO Nepal and the World Diabetes Foundation fund FCHV programmes already. We approach them as co-implementation partners, not just funders. Their project grants flow in 6–9 months, independent of municipal procurement cycles. We use the free pilot period to generate outcomes data, so when municipality budget season opens in Ashad, we walk in with a proven case, not a pitch deck.
"Grant-first to build. Donor projects to bridge. Municipal SaaS to scale. Three separate timelines — we don't mix them up."
BusinessMedium
"What's your unit economics? Cost to acquire one municipality client?"
~20 sec▾
Answer
Customer acquisition cost for a municipality is roughly NPR 80,000–1.2 lakh: one 3-day FCHV training event (NPR 25,000), two field visits for onboarding (NPR 20,000), and roughly 2–3 months of a BD person's time at NPR 50–60K/month prorated. Against an annual contract of NPR 5–8 lakh, that's a CAC payback of under 3 months. Once onboarded, churn risk is very low — switching costs are high because FCHV retraining and data migration are painful. We're targeting an LTV:CAC ratio above 10x by Year 2.
"CAC under NPR 1.2L. Revenue NPR 5–8L/year. Payback in 3 months. LTV:CAC above 10x. The unit economics work."
BusinessMedium
"You're heavily donor-dependent. What happens when the WHO grant ends or WDF moves to a new priority?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
We treat every donor project as a municipal sales pipeline. Every district we enter through a WHO or WDF grant, we exit with 3–5 signed municipal SaaS contracts. By Year 3, our target is 50%+ of revenue from government SaaS — donor funding cross-subsidises expansion into new districts but doesn't prop up existing operations. We also deliberately spread across multiple donors: WHO, WDF, UNICEF, GIZ, and bilateral sources like USAID's Digital Health Activity. No single donor represents more than 35% of revenue by Year 2. Donor dependency is a Year 1 reality — it's a Year 3 risk we actively design against.
"We use donor projects to enter new districts and sign municipal contracts. Donors fund growth, not survival."
BusinessEasy
"When do you break even?"
~15 sec▾
Answer
Month 30 — conservatively. That's with 8–12 municipalities on annual contracts plus active donor project revenue. Monthly burn by then is NPR 2–2.5 lakh. Monthly revenue from 10 municipalities at NPR 6 lakh average annually is NPR 5 lakh/month — plus donor project income. We cross break-even in the second half of Year 2 and are meaningfully profitable by Year 3 with 25–35 municipalities. We're not chasing break-even early at the cost of growth — we want to be in 30 municipalities before we optimise margins.
"Break-even Month 30. Profitable Year 3. Growth before margin optimisation."
Market & competition Q7 – Q12 · ~3 minutes
MarketHard
"India already has the NCD-GoI app with 1 million+ downloads. Why won't India just expand into Nepal and kill you?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
India's NCD-GoI app is actually our best asset — because it proves the model works and documents exactly what fails. Published evaluations show it requires consistent internet — it crashes in low-bandwidth environments. It's built for ASHA workers on India's ABHA digital ID system. None of that infrastructure exists in Nepal. India has never once expanded a government health app across its border — their own rollout took 8 years and is still incomplete in half their districts. Nepal is a sovereign country with its own MoHP, NHMIS, FCHV cadre, and procurement rules. An Indian government app without local government partnership, Nepali-language UX, and offline-first architecture is unusable here. They're not our competitor. They're our reference case.
"India's app proves the model. Their documented failures in offline environments and language localisation are the exact problems we exist to solve."
MarketHard
"What stops Medic Mobile or CommCare — who already work with FCHVs in Nepal — from adding an NCD module and making you irrelevant tomorrow?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
Nothing stops them technically — and that's the right question. Our defence is not technical exclusivity. It's speed, focus, and depth. Medic Mobile is a platform for building health apps — not a product. Adding an NCD module means their team scoping, designing, and building for Nepal's FCHV-NCD workflow specifically. Their last Nepal-focused feature took 18+ months from conception to field deployment. We'll have our pilot running in 8 months. By the time they ship an NCD module, we have 200 FCHVs trained on our app and our data in the NHMIS pipeline. Switching costs are high — retraining 200 FCHVs and migrating patient data is not a decision a municipality makes lightly. First-mover advantage in this market is durable because the users are sticky.
"Speed and focus beat platform breadth. We'll have 200 trained FCHVs and NHMIS integration before they finish scoping their NCD module."
MarketHard
"You have zero traction. No users, no revenue, no signed contracts. Why should I believe anyone will actually use this?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
We're pre-launch — no traction yet. But we have three things that replace traction at this stage: validated demand, a ready user base, and a warm pipeline. On demand: a published PLOS One study shows FCHVs achieved 94.5% accuracy in CVD risk screening with simple tools — the capability is proven. On users: 168 FCHVs trained by WDF/NeDS in Gandaki Province are our immediate pilot cohort — that's not cold outreach, that's a warm handshake. On pipeline: one municipality has expressed interest in an MOU for a free pilot. We're not guessing at demand. We're responding to an existing, documented gap that MoHP itself acknowledges in the 2026–2030 NCD Action Plan.
"No product traction yet. Validated demand, 168 warm FCHVs, and a municipality MOU in progress. That's what pre-launch looks like when done right."
MarketMedium
"Nepal has had 30+ governments in 30 years. Political instability will kill your government sales pipeline."
~20 sec▾
Answer
We sell to municipalities, not the federal government. Nepal's 2017 federalisation was designed precisely to insulate local services from central political instability. A change in the federal cabinet does not cancel Pokhara Metropolitan City's ward-level health contracts. Additionally, NCD data reporting is a constitutional obligation — municipalities must submit it regardless of which party governs. We're the compliance infrastructure, not a discretionary programme. Our donor revenue — WHO, GIZ, WDF — is completely independent of Nepali politics. Central instability actually strengthens our case: municipalities want tools that work regardless of what Singha Durbar does.
"753 autonomous municipalities, not one federal government. Federalisation is our political risk hedge."
MarketMedium
"How big is the actual addressable market? Give me a number."
~20 sec▾
Answer
Nepal alone: 753 municipalities × NPR 6 lakh average = NPR 45 Cr SaaS TAM. Add 7 province tiers at NPR 25 lakh each = NPR 1.75 Cr. Add MoHP national license = NPR 50 lakh. Nepal TAM: roughly NPR 50 Cr annually. Our realistic serviceable market in 5 years — 30% penetration of municipalities plus province tiers — is NPR 15–18 Cr. For South Asia regional licensing: Bangladesh alone has 80,000+ community health workers. At $15K per country deployment across 8 countries in the region, that's a $120K one-time licensing pool growing into annual maintenance contracts. Combined Nepal + regional SAM in Year 5: roughly $3–4M ARR.
"Nepal TAM is NPR 50 Cr. Realistic 5-year SAM is NPR 15–18 Cr domestically plus $2–3M regional. Not unicorn scale — but a very profitable, defensible business."
MarketMedium
"Why hasn't this been built already? If the problem is so obvious, why is no one solving it?"
~20 sec▾
Answer
Three reasons. First, timing: Nepal's federalisation only happened in 2017 — municipalities only got independent health budgets 7 years ago. The procurement infrastructure for a product like this didn't exist before. Second, market attractiveness: global health tech companies see Nepal as too small and too low-margin. They go to India, Kenya, Nigeria — larger markets. That's our protection, not our problem. Third, capability gap: building offline-first, Nepali-language, low-literacy UX for FCHVs requires deep local knowledge that outsiders don't have. The combination of federalisation creating the budget, the NCD burden creating the urgency, and smartphone penetration among FCHVs finally reaching critical mass — this window is 2024–2026. We're in it.
"Federalisation created the budget in 2017. Smartphone penetration reached FCHVs by 2022. The window is now, not 5 years ago."
Technology & product Q13 – Q18 · ~3 minutes
TechHard
"Offline sync across thousands of cheap Android phones in rural Nepal is technically very hard. Have you actually built this or is it still a slide?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
We're using a proven, production-tested stack — not inventing new technology. Flutter + Drift (SQLite) on device as the primary database. Every write generates a UUID-stamped sync queue entry. Workmanager runs a background job every 15 minutes — when connectivity appears, even 2G for 30 seconds, it pushes compressed JSON batches to Supabase on AWS Singapore. This is the identical pattern that Medic Mobile uses across 60,000+ community health workers in Africa and Asia — including Nepal's own maternal health FCHV programme right now. Conflicts use timestamp-based last-write-wins for single-source data. The hard part isn't the sync architecture — it's device fragmentation. We test on 10+ Android models, target APK under 40MB, and pre-cache all Nepali voice prompts at install.
"Same stack Medic Mobile runs for Nepal's FCHVs today. We're not reinventing offline sync — we're applying it to NCD."
TechHard
"A patient has a stroke because your CVD risk algorithm gave the wrong score. Who is liable — you, the FCHV, or the municipality?"
~25 sec▾
Answer
The algorithm we use is the WHO PEN protocol — the same one MoHP has officially adopted and trains FCHVs to apply manually today. We're not introducing a new clinical protocol. We're automating one that's already government-endorsed. The app doesn't diagnose or prescribe — it generates a risk score and a referral recommendation. The clinical decision sits with a nurse or doctor at the health post, as it does today. A published PLOS One study shows FCHVs achieve 94.5% agreement with doctors using this protocol manually — removing the manual calculation step reduces error, it doesn't introduce it. On formal liability: Nepal has no specific health app legislation yet. We carry professional indemnity insurance and our terms of use are reviewed by a health law advisor before launch. The baseline we're replacing is no screening at all — that's the real liability.
"We automate a WHO protocol FCHVs already use. The algorithm reduces human calculation error. The clinical decision remains with licensed staff."
TechMedium
"Patient health data on cheap Android phones is a privacy disaster waiting to happen. Lost phone = thousands of records exposed."
~20 sec▾
Answer
Three layers. Minimal data collection: we store name, age, ward number, BP/glucose, risk score — no photos, no national IDs, no addresses. Less than what's already on paper registers. AES-256 encryption via SQLCipher on the local database — a stolen phone cannot be read without the PIN. Remote wipe: the supervisor dashboard can trigger a database purge on next sync if a phone is reported lost. Also: the alternative is paper registers kept in homes, left at health posts, sometimes lost in floods. We are materially improving privacy compared to the status quo, not degrading it.
"Encrypted local DB. Remote wipe capability. And we collect less data than the paper register sitting in that FCHV's bag right now."
TechMedium
"You claim FHIR and DHIS2 integration. Those are complex standards. Is this real or slide-ware?"
~20 sec▾
Answer
Precise answer: we export FHIR R4 bundles on demand — we don't build a FHIR-native internal data store, which would be over-engineered for a field app. For DHIS2: it has a well-documented REST API that accepts JSON. Our data schema is already mapped against NHMIS data elements — the actual integration is 4–6 weeks of backend work. That's a Month 9–12 feature, not an MVP feature. In the pilot, supervisors export a CSV that health officers upload manually. That's enough to prove value and earn the government contract that justifies building the full API integration. I won't claim DHIS2 integration is live on Day 1 — it isn't. But the path is clear and the technical lift is 4–6 weeks, not 6 months.
"FHIR export, not FHIR store. DHIS2 REST API integration is 4–6 weeks of backend work in Month 9. MVP uses CSV export — good enough to earn the contract."
TechMedium
"FCHVs are in their 30s–50s with limited digital literacy. What makes you think they'll actually use your app?"
~20 sec▾
Answer
FCHVs haven't resisted digital tools — they've been handed tools designed without them. The Medic Mobile Community Health Toolkit deployed to 10,500 FCHVs in Nepal for maternal health achieved strong adoption precisely because it was offline-first, Nepali-language, and co-designed in the field. That's our exact design approach. Our UX rules: maximum 3 taps for any core action, full Nepali voice guidance on every screen, colour-coded risk outputs that communicate meaning without reading. We're conducting co-design FGDs with FCHVs in Kaski before writing a line of code. And we use a peer-champion model — train 5 "super FCHVs" per ward who support their peers — which is proven in FCHV maternal health adoption. The willingness is there. The barrier is bad design, and we're solving that.
"Medic Mobile proved FCHVs adopt digital tools when they're co-designed. 3 taps maximum. Nepali voice. Colour-coded outputs. Same playbook."
TechEasy
"Why Flutter? Why not a web app that works on any device?"
~15 sec▾
Answer
Web apps cannot reliably access device-level offline storage, background sync, push notifications, and Bluetooth (for future BP device integration) the way native apps can. A progressive web app in a browser tab that FCHVs accidentally close mid-screening is not acceptable in a clinical workflow. Flutter gives us near-native performance on cheap Android devices, a single codebase, strong offline storage support via Drift, and a large pool of Nepali developers who know it. It's the right tool. The supervisor dashboard, where connectivity is reliable, is a web app — right tool for that context too.
"Web apps can't do reliable background sync and offline-first at this level. Flutter for the field. Web for the office. Right tool for each context."

RiskHard
"What's the single biggest reason this fails? Be honest."
~20 sec▾
Answer
Adoption without sustained training support. The technology risk is manageable. The market risk is manageable. The existential risk is building a good product that FCHVs use for 2 weeks and then abandon because there's no one reinforcing the behaviour change. This is what happened to several FCHV digital pilots in Nepal before us. Our mitigation is baked into the cost model: we budget for a peer-champion model — 5 trained super-FCHVs per ward, monthly supervisor check-ins, and in-app nudges tied to sync data. If FCHVs stop syncing, supervisors see it immediately and intervene. We don't build adoption once and walk away. But I want to be honest: this is the hardest thing to do at scale, and it's the thing I watch most carefully.
"Adoption without sustained support is the existential risk. Peer champions, supervisor alerts, and in-app nudges are our mitigation — but it's the thing I worry about most."
Why this answer works
Investors respect founders who know their real risk. Saying "the technology might not work" or "government might not buy" signals you haven't thought deeply. Saying "adoption at scale without support" shows you understand the human dynamics of health systems — which is exactly the sophisticated answer that earns trust.

RiskHard
"Last question. Give me one reason — one specific, concrete reason — this is a business and not just a well-intentioned idea that never makes money."
~30 sec▾
Answer — your closing punch. Deliver this with eye contact. Don't rush it.
The budget already exists and is already being spent badly.

Every municipality in Nepal allocates NPR 5–15 lakh per year for NCD screening — paper registers, manual data collection, FCHV stipends. That money leaves their account every Falgun and produces reports that arrive at MoHP two months late, half-empty, and filed in a drawer. I'm not asking any municipality to find new money. I'm walking into a room where NPR 5 lakh is already committed to a broken process and offering to make that same NPR 5 lakh actually work — with real-time data, automated NHMIS reports, and measurable BP control outcomes they can show their constituents.

The sales question is not "will you pay for a health app?" It's "will you pay us instead of your current broken system?" That is a fundamentally different question. And the answer is yes.
"We're not creating a new budget. We're replacing a broken one. The money is already in the room."
After this answer — stop talking.
This is your close. Say it, make eye contact, and go silent. Do not add "so yeah, that's kind of why..." or "I hope that answers your question." Land it and let it sit. Silence after a strong close is power.

The question behind every question

Every investor question is actually one of four underlying fears. Know which one they're really asking:

Business model questions → "Can this make money or is it a charity?" Always anchor on existing government spend being redirected, not new spend being created.

Market questions → "Is this real demand or manufactured demand?" Cite the PLOS One study, existing WDF programmes, MoHP's own NCD Action Plan. You're not creating a problem — they already know they have it.

Technology questions → "Is this real or vaporware?" Name the specific library. Name the specific precedent. Specificity signals credibility.

Risk questions → "Why will this fail?" Never get defensive. Acknowledge the risk honestly, name the mitigation concretely, then reframe toward the broken baseline you're replacing.
